Reporting Bias

This page provides the details underpinning an example of reporting bias. This example focuses on one clinical recomendation, topical NSAIDs versus placebo for non-back pain over 1-7 days, from a larger, very well-done clinical practice guideline (PMID 32805126). The goal of this example is to demonstrate the need for authors of guidelines and evidence syntheses to provide two rates that describe the possibility of publication bias their underlying evidence base:
  • Registration rate of published subjects (RATEregpub)
  • Publication rate of registered subjects (RATEpubreg)
This example has been presented at the 2022 Annual Meeting of the Society of General Internal Medicine (see Notes below).

Contents

  • Publication rate of subjects registered at ClinicalTrials.gov

Publication rate of subjects registered at ClinicalTrials.gov

Total published and registered in 2 studies: 314
Total registered in 13 studies: 3474 = 2889 + 314 + 271
Rate of publication of registered subjects: 9% (314/3474 subjects)

Subjects: 2889 (3202 [per studies in ACP Appendix 2] - 313 in GlaxoSmithKline)

We found one additional registration that has not been published

Subjects: 271
      3. Hamelsky S. Efficacy and Safety of Diclofenac Diethylamine 2.32% Gel in Patients With Acute Ankle Sprain. 2010 PMID = NA (not published) NCT00573768. Negative results. Not found in ACP search.
Note:
  • The 314 subjects were in the 2 trials (8% of registered subjects) that were published showed benefit of NSAIDs.
  • Two trials that were registered but not published posted negative results at ClinicalTrials.gov (Lau, 2014 NCT02324270; Hamelsky, 2010 NCT00573768)
  • The twelve registered trials without results were registered by industry between the years 2007 and 2019 (median year of registration 2009).

Excluded by APC from pairwise analyses but may be helpful

      1. Lai PM, Collaku A, Reed K. Efficacy and safety of topical diclofenac/menthol gel for ankle sprain: A randomized, double-blind, placebo- and active-controlled trial. J Int Med Res. 2017 PMID 28345425; NCT02100670. NSAID not better than menthol, but ACP excluded as did not have true placebo.
      2. Serinken M, Eken C, Elicabuk H. Topical Ketoprofen Versus Placebo in Treatment of Acute Ankle Sprain in the Emergency Department. Foot Ankle Int. 2016 PMID 27198132; NCT02393846. NSAID superior at 30 minutes, but ACP excluded as did not report pain after 30 minutes.
